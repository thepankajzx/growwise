The Problem It Solves

Readers finish The Psychology of Money fully convinced that behavior beats intelligence, that "enough" matters, that room for error is sacred. Then, three weeks later, they impulse-upgrade their phone, chase a hot stock, or say yes to a lifestyle expense they can't undo — and only recognize after the fact that it was ego, fear, or comparison talking.

The book explains the psychology beautifully in hindsight. It gives no tool for the moment the decision is actually being made.

Why Existing Advice Isn't Enough

Budgeting rules (50/30/20, envelope systems) check the math of a decision but never the psychology behind it. The book's own stories — Ronald Read the janitor, the Vanderbilts — are memorable but not operational. There's no lightweight, repeatable checkpoint a person can run in real time, standing in a showroom or staring at a trading app, that forces the invisible driver into view before the money leaves the account.

Why This Framework Exists

To turn the book's philosophy into a pre-decision ritual — a psychological pre-flight check, not another mental model to admire and forget.

Core Philosophy

Every financial decision has two currents running under it: the Visible Reason (what you tell yourself) and the Invisible Driver (the old money story, the ego, the comparison, the fear). This framework surfaces the second one before the money moves.

Real-Life Example

Someone sees a colleague post about a crypto coin up 40% this week and opens the app to invest a bonus.

  • Story Scan: 10/25 — "I grew up broke, this feels like my one shot"
  • Ego Scan: 8/25 — "I want to tell friends I got in early"
  • Margin Scan: 15/25 — losing it would hurt but not ruin them
  • Future-Self Scan: 12/25 — likely regret
Total: 45/100 PAUSE 24 HOURS

Next day, calmer, they invest a smaller amount deliberately.
